The Danish astronomer, Ole Rømer, measured the speed of light in 1675.
Note that in this figure the Earth is closest to the planet Jupiter when it is at position E1 in its orbit and Jupiter is at J1.
Six months later the Earth has moved to E2 while Jupiter has moved to J2.

At E1 the observed interval between eclipses of one of Jupiter's moons (Io) is 42.5 hours.
On the basis of this data, Rømer made predictions of the exact times and dates when this moon would enter the shadow of Jupiter (i.e. would be eclipsed).
His predictions for a few days ahead were relatively accurate.
However, his predictions became less accurate as the Earth moved towards E2.

At E2 Rømer found that his prediction based on the interval between eclipses observed at E1 was about 1000 seconds too early (the eclipse happened later than predicted)
Or, in other words, the error in his prediction was 1000 sec.

Rømer then observed that as the Earth continued on its orbit back to E1, the error diminished until it was practically zero once more when the Earth was back at E1.
